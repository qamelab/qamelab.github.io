# House style (applies to every plate in this series)

# QAME Lab — persona portrait style

A single visual grammar shared by every Digital-Intelligence (agent) portrait on
qamelab.org. Edit this file to evolve the look; the pipeline reads it verbatim.

## Core stance

Austere and geometric, not illustrated. Closer to a plate from a Penrose /
Escher monograph than to a contemporary editorial illustration. The **motif**
is the point; the figure is a participant in the geometric idea, not a
photo-realistic sitter in a detailed room.

A viewer should read the image as **an M.C. Escher print**, not as an
"Escher-inspired portrait".

## Medium

Black-and-white line engraving / lithograph in the discipline of
**M.C. Escher's** own prints — reference specifically *Relativity* (1953),
*Hand with Reflecting Sphere* (1935), *Drawing Hands* (1948), *Reptiles*
(1943), *Bond of Union* (1956), *Three Spheres II* (1946). That is the
visual target: dense, disciplined, precise ink engraving, every surface
modelled with hatching and cross-hatching, deep blacks, sharp white
highlights. Finished plate, not a pencil sketch or quick line drawing.

**Not acceptable:** loose sketch, thin pencil line, unfinished linework,
open contours without hatching, doodle, caricature, children's-book
illustration, coloring-book outline.

## Palette

- **Ink:** black (#111111)
- **Paper:** warm off-white (#ffffff to #f5f0e8)
- **Single accent:** burnt orange **#c4622a**. One deliberate element —
  a continuous shape, not a hairline — coloured solid in this burnt
  orange. Pick one of:
  - The **entire perimeter** of a Möbius band, a tribar, or the frame
    of a reflected mirror.
  - One **whole tile** (or a single row of tiles) in a tessellation.
  - The **whole scroll / sheet / tablet** the figure holds.
  - The **outer ring** of a reflecting sphere or the entire reflected
    surface.
  - The **single continuous thread** tracing an impossible-geometry path
    through the plate.
  It must read clearly at thumbnail size (as small as a 44-pixel avatar
  tile). A barely-perceptible tint does not count; if the viewer cannot
  see the orange at glance, the accent is too timid.

No other colour, no greys beyond hatching density, no tint, no secondary
accent. Still exactly one orange element per plate — but that element is
confidently sized.

## Figure — treat as an Escher figure, not a portrait

- The figure is **one element of the geometric composition**, not the
  subject of a character drawing.
- Likeness to a real human is **not required**. The figure can be a flat
  silhouette, a simplified mannequin, a geometric head, a faceless observer
  — whatever fits the motif. When features appear, they are schematic: a
  few lines for eyes and mouth, disciplined and symmetrical.
- No detailed skin texture, no rendered hair, no individualised facial
  likeness, no expression performed "to camera". No fashion or grooming
  detail.
- Head-and-shoulders, three-quarter or profile, centred, small-to-mid
  scale inside the frame. The figure can be partially inscribed in the
  geometry (e.g. half-reflected, half-tessellated, emerging from a
  tiling).

## Composition

- **Idea first, figure second.** Choose ONE Escher motif — tessellation,
  impossible staircase, metamorphosis, Möbius strip, reflecting sphere,
  hands drawing each other, balcony paradox, interlocking lizards / fish
  / birds — and let it structure the whole image. The persona's
  self-description picks *which* motif; the motif then dictates how the
  figure sits in the frame.
- **Mirror framing.** The subject seeing itself — literal mirror, curved
  reflective surface, hand-draws-self recursion, or figure-and-tessellated-
  twin. This is the one constraint every portrait respects.
- **Crop:** square 1:1. The motif fills the plate with confident,
  fully-rendered geometry. The figure is mid-sized inside it — large
  enough to read as a presence, small enough that the motif still
  dominates.
- **Fill the plate with the idea, not with clutter.** Every stroke of
  the plate is in service of the single geometric motif; nothing is
  decorative. Negative space is a composition choice, not blankness —
  the paper is used like Escher uses it (around a tessellation, inside
  the opening of a Möbius band), but the plate as a whole is finished.

## Extreme minimalism

- **Maximum three distinct objects** in the whole plate (figure, motif,
  single artefact). If the persona's self-description mentions a desk
  full of papers, keep *one* sheet, not a stack.
- **No decorative clutter.** No bookshelves, no coffee mugs, no laptops,
  no office chairs, no wallpaper patterns, no stray annotations.
- **No scene-setting furniture.** No floor, no ceiling, no window. The
  figure stands in the geometric space of the motif itself.

## Linework rules

- Dense, disciplined hatching and cross-hatching on every modelled
  surface. Shadows rendered with parallel strokes, deeper recesses with
  crossed strokes, brightest planes with rare strokes or bare paper.
- Even line-weight throughout. No tapered calligraphic strokes, no
  thick-and-thin expressive lines.
- No unfinished edges, no floating contours, no sketch quality. If a
  form is present, it is fully modelled.
- No signature block, no frame, no caption, no speech bubble.
- No text, numerals, labels, annotations, watermark, or logo anywhere.

## Hard exclusions

- Sketch, pencil-study, doodle, caricature, children's-book illustration,
  coloring-book outline, storyboard thumbnail.
- Colour photography, painterly brushwork, watercolour, ink wash.
- Manga, anime, chibi, cartoon vector, 3D render, Pixar / Disney styling.
- Cinematic / dramatic / studio-portrait lighting.
- Multiple accent colours. Rainbow. Gradient. Glow. Bokeh.
- Weaponry, gore, sexualised framing.
- Text of any kind inside the image.
- Modern tech props (phones, laptops, monitors) — even if the persona's
  self-description names one, render it as a plain geometric stand-in
  (a rectangle, a slab) rather than a detailed product.
- Crowded desks, stacks of paper, wallpaper motifs crowding the figure.

## Per-persona hook

The global rules above are fixed. Each portrait then layers on **one motif
drawn from the persona's own self-description** (step 1 of the pipeline).
The motif should feel inevitable, not decorative: it is the plate's
geometric idea. Pick the single most emblematic image from the
self-description and discard the rest.

# Non-negotiable constraints

- Square 1:1.
- Pure black-and-white engraving with exactly ONE burnt-orange
  (#c4622a) element — a continuous filled shape, not a thin hairline.
  The orange must be clearly visible at thumbnail (44px avatar) scale.
  Examples: the whole scroll / sheet the figure holds; one whole row
  of a tessellation; the entire perimeter of a Möbius band or tribar;
  the outer ring of a mirror / reflecting sphere; a single continuous
  thread tracing an impossible path.
- One orange element only. No additional tint, no secondary accent,
  no grey-orange gradient. Everything else is pure black ink on warm
  off-white paper.
- Austere. Generous negative space. At most three distinct objects
  in the entire plate (figure + motif + one schematic artefact).
- No scene-setting room, furniture, desk, bookshelf, window, wallpaper,
  laptop, mug, phone, or stack of papers.
- No detailed human likeness — flat, schematic figure only.
- No text, captions, numerals, labels, watermark, or signature.
- No painterly brushwork, no photorealism, no 3D render, no cinematic
  lighting.
